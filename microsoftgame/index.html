Game Design Project

UX design internship at Microsoft

This is the project that I worked on during my internship at Microsoft Foundry. I was a UX designer in a team with four developers and one project manager. Together, we designed and developed the game that shipped in Beta on the Microsoft store. Throughout the internship we used agile scrum development methodology which entailed sprint planning, task weighting and delivering end-to-end user experiences at the end of each sprint.

My role: designing the game mechanics, prototyping interaction, designing user workflows, designing the character and the game story, designing and producing visual assets for the game, user-testing.

Problem

Most applications made for Microsoft Band are designed for health and activity tracking. However, the device’s sensors have untapped potential for a variety of other use cases. What could be the next out-of-the-box Microsoft Band experience?

Solution

The Band’s sensors, such as accelerometer and gyroscope capture enough data to detect certain gestures from the hand’s movement. Our solution proposes using Microsoft Band as a controller for a game displayed on the phone or tablet screen. The gaming experience includes three mini-games, activated by three different gestures. The mini-games repeat one after another, every time with increased challenge.

User research

As a designer on the team I was in charge of conducting the initial user research and seeing how our concept proposition could match the needs of target user group. Surveys and interviews have revealed some useful trends among Microsoft Band users.

  • Most of them identify themselves as casual gamers
  • They enjoy fast-paced performance-oriented games
  • They play games 10 -20 minutes on average

Having gathered and analyzed the data, we’ve proposed to create an experience composed of multiple mini-games activated by different gestures. This proposition was justified by the assumption that mini game stages would give the user the necessary sense of gratification even after playing for short periods of time. The idea of mini games also tied well with different gestures (one gesture for one mini game).

Story and character design

Inspired by color books for adults, the game invites the player in a virtual color book with a cute paint droplet as a main character. The animations below are short extracts from the intro cutscene to the game that I created.

I was responsible for designing and producing all animations and visual assets for the game. When designing and animating the Droplet, I aimed to create a lively and jumpy character that the player could relate to. Although it doesn’t have any facial features, I animated the Droplet’s body, hands, and legs to create a sense of a fun little cartoon creature.

The droplet of paint needs to overcome various obstacles and challenges to ultimately achieve the goal of coloring in a drawing. The player’s job is to help the paint droplet through all the stages of the journey.

UX challenges: communicating gestures

One of the key UX design challenges I tackled in this project was the effective communication of gestures. Microsoft Band is not commonly used as a gesture controller, which is why users needed a comprehensive tutorial to be able to start the gaming experience easily and smoothly.

I worked on multiple iterations and user-tested my designs weekly before I could find the right solution to the problem. Neither text instructions, nor static illustrations of gestures seemed to work.

After many playtests I determined that the most intuitive way to teach the player a certain gesture, is to provide an animated illustration of the gesture and let the player tap the screen to indicate that they are ready to play after processing the instructions completely.

Final Outcome

Over twelve weeks we designed and developed a gesture-controlled mobile game thereby taking the project from a mere idea to the version that was ready to be shipped to the store.
